Types of Negotiation


Types of Negotiation

Negotiation is a process of communication between two or more parties to reach an agreement. It is a process of problem-solving and decision-making that can be used in many different contexts. There are several different types of negotiation that can be used in different situations.

1. Distributive Negotiation: This type of negotiation is also known as win-lose negotiation. It is a competitive approach to negotiation where one party’s gain is the other party’s loss. This type of negotiation is often used when the parties are trying to divide a fixed amount of resources.

2. Integrative Negotiation: This type of negotiation is also known as win-win negotiation. It is a cooperative approach to negotiation where both parties can benefit from the outcome. This type of negotiation is often used when the parties are trying to create a new solution that is beneficial to both parties.

3. Competitive Negotiation: This type of negotiation is also known as positional negotiation. It is a confrontational approach to negotiation where the parties are trying to gain the upper hand. This type of negotiation is often used when the parties are trying to gain the most favorable outcome for themselves.

4. Collaborative Negotiation: This type of negotiation is also known as interest-based negotiation. It is a cooperative approach to negotiation where the parties are trying to find a solution that is beneficial to both parties. This type of negotiation is often used when the parties are trying to find a mutually beneficial solution.

5. Principled Negotiation: This type of negotiation is also known as problem-solving negotiation. It is a cooperative approach to negotiation where the parties are trying to find a solution that is fair and reasonable. This type of negotiation is often used when the parties are trying to find a solution that is in the best interest of both parties.

By understanding the different types of negotiation, individuals can use the most appropriate type of negotiation for their particular situation. This will help them to reach an agreement that is beneficial to both parties.

Stages of Conflict:

Conflict typically follows a predictable pattern, known as the stages of conflict. These stages include:

1. Latent Conflict: This is the stage in which the potential for conflict exists, but has not yet been expressed.

2. Perceived Conflict: This is the stage in which the conflict is recognized and acknowledged by the parties involved.

3. Felt Conflict: This is the stage in which the parties involved begin to experience the emotional and psychological effects of the conflict.

4. Manifest Conflict: This is the stage in which the conflict is expressed openly and the parties involved attempt to resolve the conflict.

5. Conflict Resolution: This is the stage in which the conflict is resolved and the parties involved move on.

Approaches to Resolving Conflict:

There are several different approaches to resolving conflict. These include:

1. Compromise: This approach involves both parties making concessions in order to reach a mutually beneficial agreement.

2. Collaboration: This approach involves both parties working together to find a solution that is beneficial to both parties.

3. Avoidance: This approach involves one or both parties avoiding the conflict in order to avoid potential negative consequences.

4. Accommodation: This approach involves one party giving in to the demands of the other in order to maintain peace.

5. Competing: This approach involves one party attempting to dominate the other in order to get their way.

Listening is an essential part of negotiation. It is important to be able to listen to the other person’s point of view and understand their needs and wants. Listening is also important for building trust and creating a positive atmosphere.

In order to be an effective listener, you need to be able to focus on the other person and pay attention to what they are saying. Here are some tips for improving your listening skills:

• Make eye contact with the other person.

• Don’t interrupt or finish the other person’s sentences.

• Ask questions to clarify what the other person is saying.

• Don’t make assumptions about what the other person is saying.

• Listen for the underlying message and feelings behind the words.

• Don’t be judgmental or critical.

• Take notes to help you remember what was said.

• Summarize what the other person has said to make sure you understand.

By improving your listening skills, you will be able to better understand the other person’s point of view and negotiate more effectively.
